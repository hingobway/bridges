Suspension Bridges and the Calculus of Cabled Bridges

Bridges are something we deal with almost every day, in all sorts of shapes and sizes. We generally don’t think much of them, but sometimes they become so massive and imposing that we can’t help but double take at how it supports weight at all.

A long time ago, when all we had were normal, bottom-supported bridges or bridges with strut supports, there were clear limits to how large such a bridge could be.

Enter the suspension bridge. This newer design for a bridge first came about in the 1800s, the basic idea being cables that run from two poles on either end of the bridge. These cables would have lots of smaller cables running vertically to the bridge, and the tension on the cable would be enough to hold up the bridge’s weight. This instantly created a number of advantages:

  • Bridges could now be much, much longer, and with far fewer supports on the ground.
  • Bridges could be built with almost no construction from underneath, because bridge segments could be hung from the cables and be stable, not needing to be attached to the other side. This meant that large rivers could stay open to boat traffic throughout construction.
  • Because of the flexibility of hanging from cable, these bridges were generally less likely to be damaged by natural disasters, and if they were, small segments can be replaced without supporting the rest of the bridge, because everything is still supported by the cables.

These benefits were clearly very helpful, because today we see suspension bridges everywhere, from the George Washington Bridge in New York City to the Golden Gate Bridge in San Francisco.
